Developmental Monitoring, Screening, and Evaluation

Parents, grandparents, early childhood providers, and other caregivers can participate in developmental monitoring, which observes how a child grows and changes over time and whether the child is meeting the typical developmental milestones in playing, learning, speaking, behaving, and moving.  The Developmental Milestones checklist below can be used for developmental monitoring.

Developmental screening takes a closer look at how your child is developing. A missed milestone could be a sign of a problem, so when you take your child to a well visit, the doctor, nurse, or another specialist might give your child a brief test, or you will complete a questionnaire about your child.  In Maryland, many licensed childcare professionals have been trained to conduct developmental screenings.  

If the screening tool identifies an area of concern, a formal developmental evaluation may be needed, where a trained specialist takes an in-depth look at a child’s development.  

If a child has a developmental delay, it is important to get help as soon as possible.

Why it's important

Developmental monitoring can be done with a simple developmental milestones checklist like the one below and can help caregivers:
* celebrate their child's development
* talk about their child's devleopment with doctors
* learn what to expect next
* identify any concerns early

Why it's important

Healthcare and childcare professionals can give children a brief test or invite a parent to complete a questionnaire to find out if a child needs more help with development.  Depending on results, a developmental evaluation can be recommended. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends screenings for all children at age 9, 18, 24 or 30 months (or whenever there is a concern).

Why it's important

A developmental evaluation is conducted by trained providers, such as the early childhood professionals with the Maryland Infants and Toddlers Program, whenever there is a concern or a suspected delay.  By completing this evaluation, a child may be found to be eligible for early intervention services -- which research has proven to be effective in addressing developmental delays and behavior concerns.
