Developmental milestones are skills such as smiling for the first time, taking a first step, and waving “bye-bye.” Children reach milestones in how they play, learn, speak, act, and move. While, the timiming of these is unique for each child, ou can watch for these developmental milestones and refer a child for help if there are any concerns.

Developmental Milestones

tracking your child's development

Children reach milestones in how the play, learn, speak, act, and move (crawling, walking, etc.).  Use the milestones listed below to see how your child grows and changes over time and whether our child meets the typical developmental milestones.  Parents, grandparents, childcare providers, physicians, and other caregivers can participate in developmental monitoring.  

how to ask for help 

If you notice that your child is not meting milestones or is exhibiting one or more of the red flags of development listed below, you can speak with your physician and request a more formal developmental screening to take a closer look at how your child is developing.  You can also call the Infants and Toddlers Program to request a developmental evaluation.

Click on the age of your child to see the milestones

Missed milestones could be a sign of a problem.  Use the brief guidelines below to help you decide if you should request a more in-depth developmental screening or evaluation.  Remember, if your child is at higher risk for developmental problems due to preterm brth, low birthweight, environmental risks like lead exposure, or other factors, you can make a referral to the Maryland Infants and Toddlers Program even if your child's development appears to be on track.

3 Months

* Turn toward bright colors and lights
* Follow moving toys or faces with their eyes
* Recognize the bottle or breast
* Respond to loud or new sounds
* Wiggle and kick with legs and arms
* Lift head and shoulders up while on stomach
* Become soothed by a familiar voice
* "Talk" back and forth with parents

6 Months

* Reach for toys and pick them up
* Roll over front-to-back and back to front
* Know faces of family members
* Responds to sounds by making sounds
* Respond when you say baby's name
* Enjoy rattles and other toys that make sounds
* Turn toward voices
* Shows curiosity; tries to get things that are out of reach

9 Months

* Copy sounds or gestures
* Reach for crumbs or other small things with their thumb and fingers
* Move toys from one hand to the other hand
* Sit unassisted
* Respond to quite talking
* Has favorite toys
* Understands "no"
* Makes lots of different sounds like "mamamama" and "bababababa"

12 Months

* Responds their name
* May be scared of strangers
* Crawls on hands and knees
* Pulls up to a standing position
* Walks by holding on to furniture
* Says 1 to 2 words
* Can point and look at familiar people or objects when asked to
* Uses voice to get your attention
* Imitates words you say
* Enjoys music

15 Months

* Uses gestures (like waving "bye")
* Likes to look at pictures in a book
* Can hold a crayon in a fist
* Can walk alne without help
* Puts out arm or leg to help with dressing
* Plays games like "peek-a-boo"
* Pokes with index finger
* Follows simple directions like "pick up the toy"

18 Months

* Likes to hand things to others as play
* Shows affection for familiar people
* Explores alne but with parent close by
* Says and shakes head "no"
* Points to show what he wants
* Points to one body part
* May walk up steps
* Pulls toys while walking

Red Flags of Development

There are red flags in a child's development that can prevent them from learning.  If you notice any of these red flags, make a referral to the Maryland Infants and Toddlers Program right away.

  • Infant
  • Toddler
  • any age
  • Awesome
  • Amazing
  • Beautiful

* Cannot bend arms or legs
* Does not smile, move or look at you when you talk or play with her
* Does not want to be held
* Does not make sounds by age 3 months
* Does not babble by age 6 months
* Shows no affection for caregivers
* Seems very floppy, like a rag doll
* Doesn't respond to sounds around him

* Temper tantrums that last 20 minutes or longer
* Hurts or bites other people
* Does not look at you when you call her name
* Does no play with toys
* Flaps hands, rocks or sways over and over
* Does not point at objects he wants
* Doesn't gain new words

* Is fussy or cries a lot, even when not tired or hungry
* Has trouble falling asleep or staying asleep
* Does not notice people
* Is unhapp most of the time
* Stops talking or babbling, loses social skills

Services are available to any eligible infant or toddler in every Maryland jurisdiction.
Referrals to the Maryland Infants and Toddlers Program can be made for children ages birth to 36 months who reside in Maryland.

online referral

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends that children be screened for general development using standardized, validated tools at 9, 18, and 24 or 30 months and for autism at 18 and 24 months or whenever a parent or provider has a concern.

Ask your child’s doctor about your child’s developmental screening.
